# Positioning

Load at the start of every positioning task. Provides the full two-phase methodology — market hierarchy first, then final positioning strategy.

**Why both phases exist:**
Phase 1 = where the brand is in the market.
Phase 2 = where it should go, what it should say, and to whom.
Phase 1 without Phase 2 = a map with no destination. Phase 2 without Phase 1 = positioning built against the wrong competitors.

---

## Phase 1 — Market Hierarchy

**Runs as part of market research (Pass 2), or as a standalone task when market research has already been completed.**

**Purpose:** Before any competitor or buyer research begins, establish where the brand sits in the market. Build a proxy-based market map using available signals rather than surveys or focus groups.

**Four proxy sources:**
- Price point relative to all identified brands in the category
- Product specifications and feature sets mapped to price tiers
- How community forums and review sites naturally tier brands
- How buyers describe tiers in their own language

**What Phase 1 produces:**

1. **Market hierarchy map** — full competitive landscape organised by tier. Use language buyers actually use (e.g. "budget," "enthusiast," "audiophile-grade"). Representative brands at each tier.

2. **Brand tier placement** — where this brand sits based on price, product, and evidence. Confidence level stated. Reasoning shown.

3. **Relevant competitor scope** — which brands are at the same tier (direct competitive input). One tier above (aspiration benchmark). One tier below (pricing pressure).

4. **Key competitive axes** — the 2–4 dimensions that define competition at this tier. These anchor the perceptual map in Phase 2.

**Platform priority:**
Web search and review sites first (they naturally cluster brands). Reddit and niche communities for buyer-language tier descriptions. Amazon category structure. Competitor Registry direct comparison tables for spec and positioning signals.

**Skills to invoke:**
- `/marketing-psychology` — after hierarchy map is drafted. Apply anchoring analysis to see how incumbents have set price/value expectations in buyer minds, and loss aversion framing to identify what buyers fear losing when switching. Sharpens competitive axes from descriptive to psychologically grounded.

**Output:** Market hierarchy map, brand placement with confidence level and reasoning, competitor shortlist, key competitive axes (with psychological grounding notes).

## Phase 2 — Full Positioning Strategy

**Runs after market research and buyer research are both complete.**

**Purpose:** Produce the brand's final positioning strategy with all evidence in hand. Starts from Phase 1. Confirms, refines, or challenges it with full data.

**Core framework:** Perceptual mapping. Plot brand and competitors on Phase 1 axes. Show where the brand sits, where competitors cluster, where white space exists.

**Output completeness standard. All three required:**
- Positioning statement (one sentence, Jobs-to-be-Done framed)
- Perceptual map (brand and competitors plotted, quadrant descriptions)
- Messaging architecture (primary message, supporting messages, language to use, language to avoid)

Output missing any of these three is not complete.

**Skills to invoke:**

`marketing:brand-review` — run first. Feed it brand-context.md and the full research package from market research and buyer research. Extract and document: brand voice attributes, tone parameters, terminology the brand owns vs. borrows, style constraints. This becomes the voice foundation the positioning statement must be written within. Do not write the positioning statement before this step completes.

`/marketing-psychology` — before constructing the perceptual map. Apply it to the white space identified — test whether the open positioning territory is psychologically compelling or just logically unclaimed. Run: social proof gap analysis (what proof exists for the white space claim), loss aversion check (what does a buyer lose by choosing this position over competitors), framing audit (is the positioning language in the brand's favour or the competitor's frame).

`/copywriting` — to construct the final positioning statement and messaging architecture. Feed it: the white space identified on the perceptual map, brand voice attributes from brand-review, buyer language map from buyer research, and psychological grounding from marketing-psychology. Use AIDA framework for messaging architecture sequence. Use PAS framework for the pain-point-led entry point. Use Jobs-to-be-Done framing for the core positioning statement.

`/humanizer` — run on the full output before writing to Notion. Positioning language that reads like AI copy defeats the purpose of the exercise.

**Output:**
- Perceptual map — brand and competitors plotted with quadrant descriptions
- Positioning recommendation — one strong paragraph, no hedging
- Positioning statement: *"For [segment], [brand] is the [category] that [differentiator] because [reason to believe]"*
- Key differentiators — grounded in competitor gaps and buyer needs
- Messaging architecture: primary message, supporting messages, language to use, language to avoid
- Buyer entry point — which segment first, which pain point to lead with, which platform to prioritise
- Confidence assessment — High / Medium / Low with reasoning
