# Presentation Narrative

Build the argument before building the slides. A beautifully designed deck with the wrong argument loses in the room. Narrative structure is confirmed with Nagi before any Figma work begins.

**Rule: narrative structure first, Figma second. Always.**

---

## The Core Principle — Answer First

State the conclusion, then support it with arguments, then support the arguments with evidence.

Never bury the answer at the end. Audiences disengage when they don't know where they're going. Every section headline should be an assertion, not a neutral topic label.

- "Revenue has grown 40% QoQ" → assertion (what the data says)
- "Revenue Trends" → topic label (forces the audience to draw the conclusion themselves)

The audience should be able to read only the slide headlines and understand the full argument.

---

## The One-Sentence Test

Every deck section must be summarisable in one sentence: *"This section proves that [specific claim]."*

If you cannot write that sentence, the logic is wrong. Not the slides — the logic. Fix the logic before designing anything.

Examples:
- "This section proves that the market opportunity is large enough to justify the investment." ✓
- "This section is about the market." ✗ (topic, not claim)

## Audience-Specific Argument Architecture

Read the brief and identify the audience before building any structure.

### Investor Deck
What is the opportunity (market size, why now)? → Why is this team uniquely positioned to capture it? → What evidence exists that the approach works (traction, validation)? → What does the investor enable?

Every slide answers one of these questions. Structure:
1. The problem (specific, named, quantified)
2. The market (size, growth, why now)
3. The solution (what it is, how it works)
4. Traction (evidence it works — numbers, not descriptions)
5. Business model (how money flows)
6. Go-to-market (how you reach customers)
7. Team (why you specifically)
8. The ask (specific amount, specific use)

### Retail Partnership Deck
Who is the customer (profile, size, motivations)? → Why does this brand belong in this channel? → What are the margin and sell-through economics? → What does partnership look like operationally?

Retail buyers read dozens of these. Lead with the commercial logic — not the brand story. Structure:
1. The customer (who buys this — specific profile that matches the retailer's customer)
2. The category opportunity (why this product category, why now, why in this retailer)
3. The product (what it is, price point, key features that matter to this channel)
4. The economics (margin, sell-through rate, expected velocity)
5. Support (marketing support, training, returns policy)
6. Partnership model (terms, exclusivity if applicable, launch plan)

### Brand Guidelines / Mediakit
Who is the brand for and what does it stand for? → What is the visual and verbal identity system? → How should the brand be applied? → What are the rules and why?

This is reference material — structure it for navigation, not narrative. Use tabs, sections with clear titles, and consistent visual hierarchy throughout.

### Internal Strategy Deck
What is the problem or opportunity? → What are the options (including doing nothing)? → What is the recommendation and why? → What are the implications and next steps?

Internal audiences want to know what to do, not be persuaded. Lead with the recommendation. Cut the persuasion layer. Structure:
1. The situation (what is true right now — facts, not opinions)
2. The complication (why the current situation requires a decision)
3. The question (what decision needs to be made)
4. The recommendation (the answer — stated first, supported after)
5. The argument (3 reasons why this recommendation is correct)
6. Next steps (specific, owned, dated)

## Slide Typology

**Assertion slide:** One claim at the top (the headline is the assertion). Evidence below. The audience reads the headline and understands the point.
- Headline: "Competitors are losing share in the $50–80 price tier" (assertion)
- Body: data, quotes, or examples that prove the assertion

**Data slide:** One primary insight, visualised. The key number is dominant. Context is secondary.
- The audience should be able to take away the insight in 3 seconds without reading an axis label
- Put the insight in the headline: "Downloads grew 3× in Q1" — then show the chart

**Comparison slide:** Two or more options evaluated against consistent criteria. Criteria on one axis, options on the other.
- Never let comparison slides become advocacy disguised as comparison — present criteria fairly, let the data argue

**Narrative slide:** Story arc, visual primary, text secondary. Use sparingly — when the emotional argument matters more than the logical one (brand story, product launch, customer story).

## Information Density Calibration

**Presented live (speaker carries context):**
- Slides are minimal — one idea, generous whitespace, large type
- The speaker provides the context; the slide provides the visual anchor
- Rule: can the slide communicate its point without the speaker? No → correct

**Read asynchronously (no speaker):**
- Slides must stand alone — more context, clearer labelling, summary annotations
- Each slide needs enough text to communicate without verbal explanation
- Rule: can someone who wasn't in the room understand this? Yes → correct

Confirm which mode applies before building. The two modes require fundamentally different density decisions.

---

## Audience Psychology in Data Visualisation

- **Investor:** "Does this support the claim?" → Key number dominant, context secondary. Don't make them work for the insight.
- **Internal team:** "What do I do with this?" → Implication foregrounded. The data is evidence for the action, not the point itself.
- **Retail buyer:** "What's the category opportunity?" → Market data before brand metrics. Show them their customer before showing them your product.
