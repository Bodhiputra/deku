## THREE CORE MESSAGES

---

### CORE MESSAGE 1 — "Actually Loud" (Volume Differentiation)

**The insight:** Volume is the #1 buyer driver in this tier. Buyers are actively
disappointed by underpowered speakers. 30W is the highest stated output in the
$40–$60 range. JBL Go 4 and Sony SRS-XB100 are approximately 5W — a 6x gap.

**The message:** "30W fills the room. Or the trailer. Or the backyard."

**Why it works:** Speaks in the buyer's situational language. Does not say
"30W output power" as a spec. Says "fills the room" — which is the outcome
the buyer actually wants. The three specific environments (room, trailer,
backyard) map directly to the three buyer contexts identified in research.

**Content expression:** Short-form video showing a compact cube playing in three
different spaces at different scales — desk in a bedroom, workshop, backyard fire.
Same speaker. Uninterrupted. The visual contrast (small cube, big sound) is the
story.

---

### CORE MESSAGE 2 — "Works Everywhere" (Connectivity Differentiation)

**The insight:** Bluetooth-only speakers create friction in real-world use.
No competitor in this tier offers quad-mode. Buyers who encounter Bluetooth
limitations have no fallback on any other $50–$60 speaker.

**The message:** "Bluetooth, AUX, TF card, USB. Four ways to play. Because
phones die, signals drop, and sometimes you just want to plug in."

**Why it works:** Uses the language of real frustration. Acknowledges the
failure modes buyers already know. Positions quad-mode not as a spec list
but as an insurance policy for uninterrupted listening. Casual, not technical.

**Content expression:** Quick demonstration of all four input modes. No narration
needed. Each mode labeled visually. The simplicity of switching modes with one
device is the story.

---

### CORE MESSAGE 3 — "Looks Like It Costs More" (Aesthetic/Value Differentiation)

**The insight:** Budget speakers look budget. The gold-trim cube with leather
strap is visually distinct from every competitor in the tier. Buyers are aware
of and frustrated by the "looks like a $20 speaker" problem. Gift buyers and
home-context buyers weight aesthetics heavily.

**The message:** "Your shelf has never looked this good for $53."

**Why it works:** Directly names the price point — which is the key conversion
signal for value-seeking buyers. Implies premium visual presence. The shelf
framing places the product in a home/lifestyle context (not a sport/adventure
context), which differentiates it from the JBL/Soundcore outdoor positioning.

**Content expression:** Product shot on a styled shelf or desk. Warm lighting.
Ambient light mode active. Leather strap visible. No background clutter.
The comparison every viewer makes mentally: "this looks like a $100 speaker."

## DIFFERENTIATION STRATEGY

### Primary Differentiators (Own these — no one else in tier has all three)
1. **30W output** — stated loudness leader by a significant margin
2. **Quad-mode connectivity** — the only product offering BT+AUX+TF+USB below $70
3. **Ambient lighting** — the only product in tier with this feature

### Secondary Differentiators (Reinforce these — real advantages, not unique)
4. **Premium aesthetic** — gold trim, cube form, leather strap — distinct visual
   identity. W-KING and Tribit have none. JBL Go 4 is minimal but not premium.
5. **Hardware EQ presets** — no app required. Direct response to Soundcore's
   app-friction problem and a comfort signal for non-tech buyers.
6. **TWS pairing** — frames a social/hosting use case. Especially relevant for
   the fire-pit gathering buyer.
7. **Hands-free calling** — practical daily-use feature. Not a headline, but
   adds real value for Marco (active user who can't stop to answer his phone).

### Acknowledged Weaknesses (Do not lead with these — manage, don't hide)
- **IPX6 vs IP67:** Honest framing as "sweat and splash resistant" is appropriate.
  Do not claim shower-proof or pool-safe. The market's IP trust gap means honest
  positioning is actually a trust-builder vs. over-claiming competitors.
- **2600mAh battery:** Modest. Do not lead with battery life. Frame as
  "rechargeable anywhere with USB-C" rather than a specific hour claim.
- **No established brand recognition:** Finecoustic is pre-launch. This is
  mitigated by the "spec stack beats brand" positioning — buyers in this tier
  have already normalized Chinese-origin brands and make decisions on features.

## BRAND VOICE DIRECTION

**Tone:** Direct, confident, self-aware. Not premium-pretentious. Not budget-
apologetic. The brand knows what it is: excellent specs in an excellent package
at a price that surprises people.

**Language to use:**
- Situational, not spec-forward: "fills your backyard" not "30W output"
- Understated confidence: "looks like it costs more" not "premium design"
- Permission-giving: "no app, no setup, just play"
- Honest about price: name the $53.80 / ~$54 directly — it's a weapon, not
  something to hide

**Language to avoid:**
- "Hi-Fi Audio" — this claim needs substantiation and will create skepticism
  without it. The buyer does not want Hi-Fi. The buyer wants "sounds great."
  Replace with: "sounds better than anything near this price."
- "Premium" as a standalone word — it reads as overreach for a $54 product.
  Earn the premium perception through the design and the price comparison.
- Audiophile terminology (soundstage, frequency response, etc.) — not the
  buyer's language. Use situational terms.
